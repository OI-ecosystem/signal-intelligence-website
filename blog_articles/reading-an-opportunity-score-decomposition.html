How to Actually Read an Opportunity Score Decomposition

21 Apr 2026 2 min read Signal Intelligence Research

An opportunity score of 0.82 is a single number. The decomposition behind it — which detectors fired, how many sources corroborated, how recent the activity is — is where the actually useful information lives.

Score range
0.0 to 1.0
Components
Detector mix + corroboration
Reading priority
Decomposition over raw score

It's tempting to treat an opportunity score as a self-contained verdict — a 0.82 sounds meaningfully better than a 0.6, and in a narrow sense it is. But the more useful information is almost always in the decomposition: which specific detectors contributed to that number, how many independent sources corroborated the pattern, and how recent the underlying activity actually is.

What a strong decomposition actually looks like

Take a hypothetical 0.82 score built from three detectors firing simultaneously — Inventory Compression, Population Scarcity, and Auction Acceleration — with three-source cross-corroboration and activity within the past 14 days. That's a structurally different, more reliable signal than the same 0.82 produced by a single detector firing in isolation with activity from a month ago, even though the headline number is identical.

Two entities can show the same opportunity score and represent meaningfully different levels of underlying confidence. This is precisely why every full signal breakdown in Trading Card Lens Weekly includes the decomposition — which detectors fired, how many sources corroborated, and how recent the activity is — rather than just the headline score.

What to look for in any decomposition

  • Detector diversity: multiple independent detector types firing together is more reliable than one detector alone, regardless of the resulting score
  • Source corroboration count: three independent sources confirming a pattern carries materially more weight than one source, even at an identical headline score
  • Recency: activity within the past two weeks is structurally more relevant than the same signal type from a month ago

Why this matters for how any score should actually be used

The headline opportunity score is a useful summary, not a substitute for reading the decomposition behind it. Anyone using this platform's data seriously should treat the full breakdown — not just the single number — as the actual evidence worth evaluating.
