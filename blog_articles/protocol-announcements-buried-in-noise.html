Anyone trying to manually follow protocol announcements across more than a handful of projects runs into the same wall quickly: there's simply too much volume, too much of it routine, and too little time to read all of it carefully enough to notice which announcements are structurally significant versus which are standard operational updates.

What gets lost in the noise, specifically

A genuinely significant protocol announcement and a routine maintenance update can look superficially similar in a social media feed — both are short, both use similar technical language, both compete for the same brief moment of attention. The difference becomes clear only when the announcement is cross-referenced against governance activity, developer momentum, and whether independent sources are corroborating its significance — exactly the kind of structured, continuous processing a human scrolling a timeline doesn't have time to do across dozens of protocols simultaneously.

This isn't a claim that automated collection is smarter than human judgment. It's the more specific claim that continuous, structured processing across many sources simultaneously catches signal that periodic, unstructured human attention — however skilled — structurally cannot keep pace with at scale.

How announcements get weighted

  • Cross-referencing announcement timing against governance proposal activity for the same protocol
  • Checking whether developer activity accelerated in the period immediately preceding the announcement
  • Source diversity — whether the announcement is corroborated independently across multiple channels, not just the protocol's own official account

The compounding effect over time

Every announcement processed this way feeds into the CIA scoring layer for its entity, contributing to a compounding history rather than being evaluated as an isolated event. An entity with a documented pattern of significant, well-corroborated announcements over time scores differently from one with a single isolated announcement, regardless of how either looked in the moment.
