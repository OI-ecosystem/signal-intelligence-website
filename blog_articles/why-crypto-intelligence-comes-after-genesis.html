Genesis Doesn't Just Score a Match. It Places You on the Timeline.

29 Apr 2026 3 min read Signal Intelligence Research

A similarity score tells you how closely two signal profiles resemble each other. It doesn't tell you where you currently sit inside that resemblance — early, late, or somewhere in the middle of a multi-year pattern. That's the question Genesis is built to answer.

HPI already answers a comparative question well: does this entity's current signal shape resemble a documented historical case, and by how much. Genesis takes that same comparison and adds a dimension HPI alone doesn't surface — position. Not just "this resembles Bitcoin's 2013 pattern" but "this sits at roughly the same stage Bitcoin was at, measured against the same case's own multi-year timeline."

What a timeline position actually means

Each of the six validated historical cases didn't arrive at recognition in a single moment — they moved through a documented sequence of stages, from the earliest detectable signal-only phase through to the point the rest of the market caught up. Genesis maps that sequence for every case as a timeline running from an early stage through to the eventual outcome, then plots where a current entity's signal profile falls along the equivalent stage of its closest-matching precedent.

This is the difference between a single static comparison and a moving one. A 47% similarity score tells you a resemblance exists right now. A timeline position tells you whether that resemblance is still early in its development or already well advanced — which is a meaningfully different piece of information.

How the comparison actually gets made

This works through CIA stage matching — comparing an entity's current Collective Intelligence Asset profile against the historical CIA profile a validated case showed at an equivalent point in its own timeline. SpaceX's CIA signature nine years before recognition looked a specific way: a certain density of cross-source corroboration, a certain novelty decay rate, a certain signal velocity. Genesis checks whether a current entity's CIA profile matches that specific stage, not just the case's resemblance in the abstract.

  • The timeline is built from the same historical replay data already validated for HPI — no separate dataset, no hindsight
  • Position on the timeline updates as new signals arrive, so the comparison reflects current activity rather than a one-time snapshot
  • The same six cases (SpaceX, Tesla, Bitcoin, NVIDIA, Shopify, OpenAI) anchor every timeline comparison, consistent with HPI's existing precedent library

Pattern archetypes: the shape behind the shape

Not every historical case moved through its timeline the same way. Genesis groups the underlying pattern into named archetypes — distinct shapes a signal profile can take on its way toward recognition, each with a different characteristic rhythm:

  • Infrastructure Builder — sustained capital and engineering deployment, visible in cases like SpaceX and Tesla
  • Ecosystem Creator — steady developer or merchant network expansion, the Shopify-style pattern
  • Network Effect Compounder — value that increases as participation increases, rather than from any single feature
  • Regulatory Beneficiary — a pattern that strengthens as the regulatory environment around it shifts favourably
  • Attention Explosion — rapid, concentrated novelty and corroboration arriving in a short window
  • Technology Inflection — the OpenAI-style shape, where a technical capability crosses a threshold that changes what's possible
  • Cultural Momentum — recognition driven by sustained cultural relevance rather than a single technical milestone
  • Scarcity Event — supply-side tightening as the primary driver, the dominant shape in Collectibles
Knowing which archetype an entity's current pattern resembles — not just which historical case — adds a layer the raw similarity score can't provide on its own. A Scarcity Event pattern and an Infrastructure Builder pattern can produce a similar headline percentage while implying a completely different kind of trajectory.

Why this stays comparative, not predictive

None of this tells anyone what will happen next for any specific entity. A timeline position is a statement about resemblance to a documented historical stage, not a forecast of a future one. The same discipline that governs every HPI output applies here without exception: this is a comparison against six real, validated precedents — never a prediction of where any current entity is headed.
